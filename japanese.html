For this, I use Rikaichan which is a Firefox plugin that translates Japanese words to English when you hover over them and Jisho which is a Japanese to English and a (kind of) English to Japanese dictionary.

(7/8/2016) Random Grammar Confusion

So, when I woke up at 11 am this morning, I was thinking of Japanese. This is something I do often: I'll just randomly think of a really simple sentence and then challenge myself to say it in Japanese. The sentence in my head was "The book was written in Japanese". This seems simple enough, right? For anyone who has been studying the language for a while, this sentence would be super easy. However, I have the Japanese language skills and literacy of a 5 year old. My initial guess for this sentence was「本が日本語で書いた」. I know, probably not right. I probably should've used the「に」particle as opposed to「で」, but I still need to do more research.

Alright, so I did a little bit of research. Research = Googling one thing by the way. It looks like I was right. "Well, why did you bother writing an entire paragraph about how you think you're wrong?", you may ask. My answer to this is simple: I want to make sure I notice mistakes, even if those mistakes aren't even mistakes in the first place. I find this important so I can make note of what I need to know and get down. So, ya, that's that I guess. I will probably be writing more later in the day after I review verb clauses again.

Ah okay so one quick thing that I just realized. The Japanese particle「で」can mean "by means of". In the context of the sentence that I was trying to form earlier, this makes complete sense.

「本は日本語で書いた。」
"The book is written by means of Japanese (language)."
At least that seems like what it would be. I also am a bit confused on the「が」vs.「は」here (still pretty new). This is because the book has a direct effect on the sentence. Wait, no.

Okay nevermind. Another thing that I get wrong only because I question my judgement. I don't think that you would need to use「が」in this case because of the whole context thing (and other stuff). For example:

「これ本はいいだ。」
"This book is good." (Probably got that wrong)
「はい、日本語で書いた。」
"Right, it was written in Japanese." (Also probably wrong)
Anyways, you get the point I guess. I don't think the「が」particle is needed in this case, but I could always be wrong.

Okay, so I was reviewing verb clauses and I thought it would be good practice to break down a sentence and just write out how I think of sentences in Japanese. Here is the sentence (taken from Tae Kim's guide on verb clauses here):

「暇な時に何をするのが好きなの？」
"What do you like to do when you're free?"
So, I'm gonna break this down into a ton of small part and then write out a big explanation on it.
First, we split it up by the main particle that joins the two parts of the sentence, the subject particle:
[verb clause/noun] +「が」+ [state of being]
Verb clause/noun =「暇な時に何をするの」
Subject particle =「が」 State of being =「好きなの」
Next, we are gonna look at the first part more closely and break that up a little bit:
「暇な時に何をするの」← Our verb clause. Let's take a bit of a closer look at it...
「暇な時」← This word means 'leisure time'.
「に」← Since 'leisure time' can be used to specify a certain span of time in which actions take place (being off of work or school, etc.), we can use the「に」particle to specify that a verb is taking place during this time.
「何」← This word means 'what'.
「を」← This is the direct object particle that we use in order to specify the direct object of the verb in our sentence. I like to think of particles as answering a certain question associated with a verb. So for this particle, I like to think of it as answering: "Well, what is it that is being [verb]-ed?". For example, if we had「食べる」(eat), we could ask the question "What is being eaten?" and then the「を」particle could answer this with "fish" if we had「魚を食べる」. This explanation is certainly not the best as different particles can do the same thing depending on whether the verb is transitive/intransitive. However, this still works for us in this case.
「する」← This is our verb of the whole clause, which means 'to do'. Now that we are at our verb, we can apply the questions we answered about this verb in full. "What time did action take place?" "During 'leisure time', as specified with the「に」particle". "What action was performed?" "Well, since this is a question, we are actually asking that ourselves, hence the「何」preceding「を」particle". Look, I know this sounds weird when I actually type it out, but this is the process that I go through in my head when breaking down a sentence (but it's not as complicated as it may seem).
「の」← This turns this whole section of our verb clause into an object that we can then use as the topic/subject of our sentences. You could also use a proper noun here, such as a person or something.

So, now we have this verb clause that we can treat as an object and not just an action taking place. We have something along the lines of "What are things done during leisure time". I'm am sorry if this does not make sense at all, but remember I am still in the very basic stage of learning Japanese myself. I usually write this stuff down so I can explain to myself what the hell is going on in my head.
Well, that was a pretty big explanation. Now, I will go over the second part of the sentence in order to bring it all together:
「好きなの？」← The state of being...
「好き」← This word is an adjective (describes a noun, but can also be used independently as it's own state of being) that means 'like' or 'desirable'. It is a na-adjective.
「な」← A particle used to attach adjectives to the nouns that they modify.
「の」← This is like a general object that we can use. I like to think of its definition as 'thing' or 'things'.
「？」← A question mark. I would hope this is known.

So, with this all broken down, we can get this sentence down to "Desirable things?". We can then pair this with the verb clause we had before.
Okay. Finally, we can pair this sentence together...
"What are things done during leisure time" +「が」+ "desirable things?"
This can then be further actually built into a coherent English sentence:
"What are things done during leisure time that are desirable things?"
And then, of course, this could be broken down further and simplified way more until you eventually get:
"What (sort of things) do you (context implies this topic) like to do during leisure time?"
Phew, that was a very long and complicated explanation of an easy sentence. It probably also made no sense whatsoever.

Also remember that, for a native speaker, they don't have to do this conversion. And that is our goal: to not have to convert sentences in our head, or go "monolingual", which is basically just reading a sentence in Japanese and immediately understanding what it means. Once this can be done with little error, you can pair this with a large vocabulary and be considered almost fluent. How cool is that. For a sentence like this, I still have to do some conversion, but it is mainly just to keep track of what I am reading throughout the sentence. As I read more and more sentences that utilize basic grammar rules like these, I notice how it gets easier for me to just understand sentences without a layer of processing it in English.

(7/9/2016) Actually Learning Vocabulary

Today I finally decided to get down to business and work on my vocabulary. After seeing a few videos mentioning Memrise, I decided I would check it out. When I first selected Japanese as the language I want to learn, it asked me whether I was a beginner or an advanced user. Of course, I selected the 'beginner' option and moved on. For the first 30 minutes of using the app, words were presented in romaji (Japanese words spelt out with Roman characters - not that beneficial). However, after a while, the app starts to take you through hiragana characters and then starts to build up actual Japanese characters from there. The problem I had with this is that it would only be teaching a few elementary words at a time and then form basic phrases out of it (i.e.「わたしはおなかがすいています。」"I am hungry."/lit."Stomach is empty."). I didn't really need this and just needed to focus on vocabulary. Another issue with this basic Japanese course is that, for the first while, there is no kanji. This is what really turned me off from the app.

After doing some searching, however, I found that there is a JLPT vocab study section. Just what I needed (no sarcasm). I started with the most basic level, JLPT 5. What each of the sections do is teach you 25 words with only hiragana/katakana and no kanji. You then review these a lot until the app makes sure you know all of them by heart. The next section is those same 25 words, but with the kanji that you would actually see had the word been in the "wild" somewhere. There is an amazing amount of review sections for both of these study sections and it is awesome. I have used it for only one day, and I know about 50 new words. I do need to review the words from time to time, though. There is a reason for this. You know how sometimes with your car or something, you can't recite the license plate number from memory, but know it when you see it? So far, this is how I am when reading Japanese. I can read words and understand them, but if I need to think of some off the top of my head, I cannot. I know that this problem with eventually disappear as I continue to study, but it is, nonetheless, annoying.

Well, that is the post for today. I am going to go study more vocab and do other things. I will continue to look for good vocab and general learning resources to use.
